Experience

Intern: Procter and Gamble

My most recent work experience was as an R&D intern with Procter and Gamble. I worked as a process development engineer, designing and optimizing manufacturing equipment for Glad trash bags. My main contribution was in the development of a finite element model of the temperature profile within a sealing process being developed for reinforced bottoms, in which I expanded our understanding of the effect of surface roughness of the film on thermal resistance. My product is scheduled to hit the shelf in JFM 2020.

P&G was a great experience because I was able to gain exposure to the operating procedures of a corporate giant. Moreover, it was an exceptional opportunity to gain cross functional understanding in fields like consumer research and brand management. In fact, if I were to return full time to the company, I would want to do so as an Associate Brand Manager so that I could be involved in the strategy, rather than the implementation of innovation.

On Campus Research: Vascular Inspired Reactor

One of my most important on campus experiences has been undergraduate research. Through working alongside PhD students and faculty, I have been able to learn through observation how best to apply scientific principles to drive learning. And while I by no means see myself pursuing a career in academia, the same principles of rigorous proof of concept and effective experimentation are applicable across all fields.

The main project I am working on is a Vascular Inspired Reactor for cell free protein expression. It utilizes a DNA based hydrogel as a scaffold for expression to occur, and transcription factors diffuse through in a continuous stream. The main area in which I took ownership was the modeling of this process for the optimization of reactor geometry, which I did via a FEM model in COMSOL. I also assisted in some of the discovery work, practicing general microbiology and biochemistry techniques like SDS PAGE, Western Blot, Bradford Assays, etc.

Cuyama Orchards: Farmers Market

My least relevant, but perhaps most formative experience was working at the Farmers Market in high school. My first year, I was paid hourly, but I quickly graviated towards earning a commission, because it was far more lucrative. In fact, as a 17 year old high school student, I was earning close to 30$ an hour on weekends selling apples.

This experience was formative in that I got to appreciate the value in owning ones work. My first weekend at the Pasadena farmers market, I took 156$ in commission. I paid my 120$ market market fee, 7$ for parking, and walked away with 29$ in my pocket. But as I began developing a customer base and building the local recognition of Cuyama Orchards as a brand - investing in wicker baskets for my display, nice tablecloths, a hygenic sampoling station - I saw the direct impact of my work on my margins. The pride I felt in something as small and simple as a farmers market stand is something I hope to feel in all work that I do.
