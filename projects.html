Projects

Oxyponics

Of everything I have worked on, Oxyponics is the project I am most proud of, and also my first iGEM project as team lead. At a surface level, the Cornell iGEM team engineered a primitive communication system between biological and mechanical systems based on different wavelengths of transmitted light. We furthermore applied this to solve a real world problem - increasing yields in hydroponic systems by controlling for oxidative stresses.

But the real reason I am proud is that we took a disorganized team, lacking in leadership and direction, and laid the foundations for success in years to come. The year before this project, Cornell iGEM was coming off of its worst performance ever at competition. This year, we won an award for overall “Best Supporting Entrepreneurship.” Moreover, for the first time since I have been on the team, we left room in the budget for long overdue capital investments and renewals of supplies.

Senior Design Project

I took my senior design class in my junior year so that I could begin a Masters in Engineering curriculum early. The class dealt with computer aided engineering - specifically, how finite difference and finite element methodology can be used to enhance engineers ability to differential equations within complicated geometries.

My project in particular involved the analysis of different blood vessel geometries with respect to heat conservation in a human finger. This follows a major theme in emergent research of emulating nature in optimizing designs for a given parameter. Specifically, from modeling the effects of fluid flow within the vasculature of the system on the convective heat loss, we were able to draw conclusions regarding the tradeoff between maximizing the insulation offered by the skin, and maximizing the arteriovenous temperature gradient so as to retain heat by countercurrent exchange.

Read my report if you are interested in learning more!

Legendairy

My first major project at Cornell iGEM was called Legendairy. In this project, we created a bacteriocin based antimicrobial to defend against bovine mastitis. My personal role was as a member of our wet lab subteam, where I was able to take ownership of work involving cloning and experimental design.

While this project didn’t do well at competition, it was a formative experience because it highlighted things that we could do better. Legendary was conceptually identical to our previous project: FishPharm. We did a fantastic job in understanding our consumer, and the science itself was executed with excellence. The problem was that instead of applying a formulaic approach to success, we needed to innovate, year after year, in order to consistently remain at the forefront of synthetic biology. This understanding was well reflected in the success of our next project, Oxyponics (see above).
